--- title: "The Chokepoint: How Communication Infrastructure Goes From Open to Extractive, Every Time" date: 2026-04-24 subtitle: "Railroads, telegraph, telephone, platforms, and what I think comes next" ---

I’m at my usual spot in HSR Layout, ground floor, been coming here long enough that they bring the coffee without asking. Kinda like this place, or perhaps working from cafes, in general. The owner, a guy I’ve spoken to maybe four times total, comes over not to talk to me specifically but just to talk, the way people do when something’s bothering them. Zomato’s increased the commission again. 28% now, he thinks. He can’t leave, he says, because that’s where the orders come from. He built the kitchen, hired the cooks, figured out the menu over years. They built the pipe. And now, through no dramatic event, no contract he remembers signing in bad faith, they own him a little. He goes back behind the counter and I sit there with my coffee going cold, thinking about how that sentence, they built the pipe, keeps showing up in history.

I’m posting this unedited. If I sit with it longer I’ll make it cleaner, and cleaner will kill what’s interesting about it right now. So here it is rough.

I started looking things up when I got home. I don’t know much history, genuinely. I studied engineering, not economics. So what follows is me going through a few cases I found, half from Wikipedia, half from things I half-remembered and then verified. I might have some details wrong. But the broad shape of what I found felt consistent enough to write down, and the shape is what I care about.

Railroads

The American railroads were built with massive public subsidy, land grants, government financing, right of way across territories the builders didn’t exactly own. The pitch was that rail would open up the continent, connect markets, lower prices, all of that. And it did. But it also meant that whoever controlled the tracks controlled who could move goods, at what cost, to which markets.

The mechanism that developed was rate discrimination. Not a fixed price for everyone, but a negotiated one based on leverage. Rockefeller, I learned, didn’t build Standard Oil primarily by being better at drilling. He negotiated secret rebates from the railroads, lower rates than competitors, and used that margin to undercut and acquire until he had enough volume that the railroads needed him more than he needed them. The infrastructure meant to open commerce became the tool that concentrated it.

What strikes me: the railroad itself looked like a neutral thing. A track is a track. But the operator of the track wasn’t neutral at all, and never really had to be.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.

Telegraph

I knew less about this one. Western Union consolidated the telegraph network in the 1860s and by the end of that decade had something close to a monopoly on the wire. They had an exclusive deal with the Associated Press, which I think meant that which stories moved fast and which moved slow was, at least partly, their call. Financial information, commodity prices, political news. Whoever routes the message also knows the message, and knowing it first is worth something.

I don’t know exactly how they monetized that asymmetry. But the structural fact of it, that owning the channel meant owning the information advantage, seems like it mattered a lot.

Telephone

This is the one I found most interesting, maybe because it’s the most complete cycle.

Bell’s patents expired in 1894. What happened next is kind of remarkable. Hundreds of local telephone companies formed across the country, rates fell, coverage spread to places the Bell system hadn’t bothered with. There was, for a while, actual competition. Then AT&T started buying up local companies and refusing to interconnect with networks they didn’t own. If you were on an independent carrier and wanted to call someone on AT&T’s network, you just... couldn’t.

There was a government agreement in 1913, the Kingsbury Commitment, that was supposed to stop the acquisitions. It didn’t really. Then there was the 1984 breakup into the Baby Bells. And then the Baby Bells spent the next two decades quietly reconsolidating. The AT&T you can subscribe to today is basically the same entity, reassembled.

The thing that gets me is the window. There was a real window, about a decade after 1894, where a genuinely different outcome was possible. Then the network got big enough that being outside it was worse than paying whatever AT&T asked, and the window closed.

Internet

The internet kept its physical layer open. TCP/IP is still a commons, anyone can build on it. The early optimists thought this was the solution, that the architecture itself would prevent capture.

What happened is that the chokepoint moved up the stack. Email didn’t get captured at the protocol level, it got captured when everyone’s email ended up at Gmail. The web didn’t get captured at HTTP, it got captured when search became how you navigate it, and then when social became how content moves through it. The infrastructure stayed open. The behavior on top of it didn’t.

The chokepoint, I think, is wherever the switching cost is highest. And that location isn’t fixed. It moves to wherever people have stopped being willing to leave.

Platforms

I don’t need to explain this one. The cafe owner knows this one.

The thing that’s different now is that the extraction is invisible. It doesn’t feel like a toll booth. It feels like the normal cost of doing business. App store fees, delivery commissions, advertising minimums to reach your own followers. Nobody thinks of these as rent, exactly. They feel like infrastructure costs, which is maybe the point.

So here’s where I am, roughly.

Openness is the growth phase. The infrastructure needs adoption before it can extract, so it presents itself as open, as a commons, as something that benefits everyone. Once adoption crosses a threshold, the chokepoint hardens. The mechanism changes each time, rate discrimination, information asymmetry, network lock-in, behavioral capture, but the shape underneath seems similar.

And there’s always a window. A period where the outcome isn’t yet determined. The window always closes. It usually looks, from inside it, like it’ll stay open longer than it does.

I keep thinking about what layer comes next. The AI systems, the physical devices they’re being built into, the way hardware and software are fusing in ways that are hard to route around. But I don’t want to write that part yet. I want to sit with the pattern first, make sure I’m seeing it right.

Maybe you’re already thinking about it. That’s kind of the point of writing this down.

I’m one person figuring this out in public. The more people thinking alongside, the better the thinking gets. Share it if it meant something to you.
