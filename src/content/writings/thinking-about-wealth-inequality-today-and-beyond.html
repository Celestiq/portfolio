--- title: "Thinking about Wealth Inequality: Today and Beyond" date: 2026-07-05 subtitle: "What Catastrophe Does That Policy Doesn't" ---

I’ve been thinking about what’s actually worth building.

Not in the abstract, sitting-around way. Something changed practically for me over the last year. With tools like Claude Code, the distance between having an idea and having a working version of it has collapsed. A thing that used to need a team and six months now takes an afternoon and some patience. Which is a strange position to be in, because it turns the hard part of building something from “can I build this” into “should I,” and that second question is a lot less comfortable to sit with.

So I started asking it directly. What’s worth spending years on. Not what’s fundable right now, or what’s trending this quarter, but what problem still matters in thirty years, the way it mattered thirty years ago. I came up with a short list, and one item kept pulling my attention back: what happens to wealth distribution once AI and robotics genuinely start doing the work people used to get paid for. Not as a thought experiment. As a live, unfolding thing.

But before I could let myself get excited about building anything in that direction, a much older and more annoying question showed up first. Has this problem ever actually been solved? Not managed, not slowed down for a decade, but solved, by people deciding to solve it, on purpose, without something terrible forcing their hand first?

I went looking for the answer and didn’t like what I found.

The historian who’s done the most thorough version of this search is Walter Scheidel, and his conclusion, stated as flatly as he states it, is this: inequality has never gone down peacefully. Not once, anywhere, in the entire recorded record. It falls only under four conditions, mass-mobilization war, revolution, state collapse, plague, and it climbs right back up the moment things get calm again. He looked for a fifth path, some case where ordinary economic development or sensible policy did the job on its own, and came up empty. His own words are worth sitting with rather than softening: there is no repertoire of peaceful mechanisms that has ever come close to what violent shocks have done.

If that’s true, it’s a genuinely uncomfortable thing to build against. It would mean the tool I’m imagining isn’t solving a hard problem but might be ignoring one.

So that’s where this essay actually starts. Not with a policy proposal, and not with a chart of billionaire wealth, though those are coming. It starts with trying to find out whether Scheidel is right, and if he is, whether there’s something underneath his four categories that a person could actually get their hands on. Because “only catastrophe works” is a conclusion, not a mechanism. And I wanted to know what’s inside the catastrophe that’s doing the work.

Scheidel’s book is called The Great Leveler, which is a slightly misleading title, because the whole point of the book is that leveling almost never happens the way we’d want it to. He goes looking across the entire span of recorded history for a case where inequality fell without something horrific causing it, and he structures the whole argument around four things he calls the Horsemen: mass-mobilization warfare, transformative revolution, state collapse, and plague. Not four examples. Four categories. According to him, that’s the complete list. Everything that has ever meaningfully compressed the gap between rich and poor falls into one of those four buckets, and nothing else has ever come close.

I want to sit with how strong that claim actually is, because it’s easy to read past it. He isn’t saying violence helps. He’s saying it’s the only thing that has ever worked, at scale, durably, anywhere. Tax policy, technological progress, education, growth itself, none of it, on its own, has a track record of doing this job. The twentieth century’s one clean counterexample, the mid-century fall in inequality across the West, gets folded right back into his framework rather than standing outside it: two world wars and a depression physically wrecked the capital stock of an entire generation of the wealthy, and everything that looks like a policy achievement afterward, progressive taxation, strong unions, welfare states, was riding on the back of that destruction, not standing on its own.

Peter Turchin’s work sits right next to this and answers a question Scheidel mostly leaves alone, which is: why does it take a catastrophe to get anyone to concede anything? His answer is that elites don’t hand back a larger share voluntarily, because there’s no internal pressure making them. As inequality rises, more people compete for a fixed number of positions at the top, and the ones who don’t make it in don’t just disappear quietly. They become a reservoir of frustrated, capable people with every incentive to challenge the order that shut them out. He calls the underlying force the wealth pump, and the pattern he traces across Rome, Han China, and early modern Europe is always the same shape: decades of quiet pressure building underneath a system that looks stable right up until it isn’t.

There’s a third piece I think belongs here too, and it answers a different question: why doesn’t inequality just correct itself once the initial cause is gone. Samuel Bowles and Mattia Fochesato went looking at the deepest possible timescale for this, using grave goods and house sizes as proxies for wealth going back ten thousand years, and what they found is that inequality stayed low for most of that span and then jumped, hard, somewhere in the Bronze Age, and never really came back down. Their explanation isn’t really about greed or ambition. It’s about the plow. Once wealth could take the form of land, worked by animals, passed to a son, something changed that had nothing to do with anyone’s personality. Wealth stopped being something a person accumulated in their own lifetime and became something that could just sit there, inherited, defended, and grown across generations, as long as two other things were also true: some kind of authority willing to protect that claim by force, and a culture willing to treat the resulting hierarchy as normal rather than an outrage. Take away either of those and the concentration doesn’t stick. Put both in place and you get something that can outlast the individual entirely.

Put these three together and you get something considerably heavier than “history is unequal.” You get a claim that inequality is not a deviation from some natural resting state of fairness that keeps trying to reassert itself. It’s closer to the resting state itself, and fairness is the deviation, one that has only ever been forced into existence by something breaking.

I didn’t enjoy arriving at that sentence. But I don’t think it’s wrong, and I’d rather build on top of something true and unpleasant than something comforting and false.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.

Once I had the four Horsemen laid out next to each other, something started to bother. War, revolution, collapse, plague. These are wildly different events. A plague doesn’t organize armies or overthrow governments. A revolution doesn’t kill a third of the population by accident. If they’re all doing the same job, that job has to be happening somewhere underneath the surface differences, not in the events themselves.

So I went looking for what each one actually does, mechanically, rather than what it is.

The plague case is the cleanest place to start. The Black Death killed something like a third of Europe, and the immediate economic effect was simple: labor became scarce, and scarce things get more expensive. Wages rose. In Western Europe, this scarcity is a big part of the story of how serfdom eventually broke down; landlords needed workers badly enough that workers could start naming better terms. That’s mechanism one, and it’s the most straightforward: when labor gets scarce relative to capital, its price goes up, and that’s not a political concession from anyone, it’s just what happens when demand outstrips supply.

Except it didn’t play out that way everywhere. In Eastern Europe, hit by the same plague, the same labor scarcity, landlords did the opposite. They tightened serfdom instead of loosening it. Which tells you something important: scarcity alone doesn’t guarantee anything. What decided which way it went was whether elites still had the coercive muscle to force people to stay put despite the market pressure in the other direction. Western elites, for reasons rooted in earlier feudal structure, mostly didn’t have that muscle anymore. Eastern elites did. Same shock, opposite outcome, because a second variable was doing the deciding.

That second variable is really about who still holds the whip hand, institutionally, when a shock hits. Revolutions and state collapse work through this channel almost exclusively. Nothing about a revolution requires scarcity or destruction in the way a plague does. What a revolution actually removes is the state’s willingness or ability to keep enforcing a rich person’s claim on their property. Take that enforcement away and the claim doesn’t mean much anymore, no matter how legally solid it was the week before.

Then there’s a mechanism that’s neither of those, and I only found it because I went looking at what actually happened to tax rates during the two world wars, rather than just accepting “war destroys wealth” as a complete explanation. Kenneth Scheve and David Stasavage looked at top tax rates across nineteen countries going back to the early 1800s, and found something specific: rates on income and inheritance jumped, often four times over, in exactly the countries running mass conscription. Their explanation is that this wasn’t really about the state needing money, though it needed that too. It was that when a government is sending ordinary citizens to die on a battlefield, the argument for letting the wealthy sit safely at home keeping their full fortune stops being an argument anyone can win in public. The compensatory logic becomes unanswerable. That’s a third, distinct lever: not scarcity, not enforcement, but the collapse of the story that made the existing distribution seem fair in the first place.

And then there’s straightforward physical destruction, which is the one people usually think of first when they hear “war destroys capital,” and which I’d put last precisely because it’s the most obvious one. Bombed factories, defaulted government bonds, requisitioned estates. The capital stock just gets smaller. Whatever wealth-to-income ratio existed before the war, it’s lower after, and everything that compounds off that ratio starts compounding from a reduced base.

Four things, then, not one thing called “catastrophe.” A shift in who’s scarce relative to whom. A weakening of whoever enforces property claims. A rupture in the story that makes the existing distribution feel legitimate. And a direct hit to the size of the capital stock itself. War tends to produce three of these at once, sometimes all four, which is probably exactly why it’s been history’s most reliable leveler. Plague mostly produces the first, and only reliably when the second one happens to be absent already. Revolution is almost pure second-and-third, delivered together, fast.

I found this genuinely clarifying, more than I expected to. Scheidel’s four Horsemen stopped looking like four different kinds of catastrophe and started looking like four different delivery mechanisms for a much smaller number of underlying variables. Which raises the obvious next question, the one I actually care about: if catastrophe works because it bundles these variables together, is there any reason a person or a policy couldn’t try to produce some of them deliberately, on purpose, without the war.

That’s the question the rest of this essay is trying to answer honestly, not optimistically.

So I had four variables instead of one vague word, and the obvious next move was to point them at the thing that made me start writing this in the first place. Not history. Now.

The way to do this is to ask, one at a time, whether any of the four are naturally present in what’s happening with AI and automation right now, the way they were present, in some combination, during a war or a plague. And I went in expecting a mixed answer. I didn’t get one.

Take labor scarcity first, since it’s the mechanism that did the most work in the Black Death case. The entire pitch of automation, stated plainly, is the opposite of scarcity. It’s abundance. A model or a robot that can do what a person used to do doesn’t make labor scarce relative to capital, it makes labor substitutable, which pushes in exactly the wrong direction. If wages rose after the plague because there were suddenly too few hands for too much work, wages are under pressure now for the mirror-image reason: there might soon be too many hands for the work that’s left. That’s not a minor difference. It means the mechanism that’s done the most historical lifting isn’t just absent here. It’s running backward.

Capital destruction is even more clearly absent, to the point where it feels almost silly to check. War shrinks the capital stock. AI investment is doing the opposite at a scale that’s hard to hold in your head, hundreds of billions of dollars a year going into compute and infrastructure, not because anyone’s rebuilding after a collapse, but because the whole point is expansion. The wealth-to-income ratio isn’t falling the way it did after 1945. It’s climbing.

The legitimacy piece is the one I went back and forth on, because I think something like it did flicker briefly during the pandemic. There was a moment, for a year or two, when the language of shared sacrifice actually showed up in public life, essential workers, we’re all in this together, that kind of thing, and it even came with a small, real, measurable effect: wages at the bottom of the labor market genuinely compressed for a while, faster than anyone expected. But it didn’t stick, and it didn’t touch the top at all. Asset prices did the opposite of what a legitimacy rupture is supposed to do; they rose, sharply, while the rhetoric of shared hardship was still in the air. Whatever that was, it wasn’t the kind of rupture that changes what a society is willing to defend. I don’t see anything like it building around automation right now, and I don’t have a good reason to expect one to show up on its own.

And the fourth variable, whether the state’s willingness to enforce concentrated ownership is weakening, might be the clearest no of all four. If anything it’s the opposite. The legal architecture around intellectual property, compute ownership, and model weights is, if anything, getting more actively defended, not less, because there’s more money riding on it than there ever was before.

So four for four, pointing the wrong way. Not one of the historical levers is turning on its own. And there’s a more specific, technical version of this same worry sitting underneath the AI research itself. The open question economists are actually chasing right now is how easily a firm can substitute AI capital for human labor without losing output, what they call the elasticity of substitution, and the early evidence leans toward that elasticity being high in exactly the industries automating fastest. If that holds, it’s not just that inequality won’t compress by accident. It’s that the r side of Piketty’s equation and the g side could be moving apart at the same time, capital’s return staying strong precisely because labor’s bargaining power is falling, which is the r-over-g gap widening for a structural reason rather than a temporary one.

I want to be honest about what this means rather than dress it up. Scheidel’s model predicts that inequality rises during peace and stability and only falls under violent disruption. What I’m looking at isn’t even ordinary peace and stability. It’s peace and stability plus a technology that happens to weaken labor’s position at exactly the moment none of the other three levers are active either. If his framework is right, this is closer to the ideal conditions for concentration than anything in recent memory, not a naturally self-correcting situation.

Which leaves exactly one option, if the goal is a different outcome than the default one. Nothing here is going to compress on its own. So the only question left is whether any of these four things can be produced on purpose, without the war, without the plague, by people deciding to build the conditions rather than waiting to be handed them by catastrophe.

That’s genuinely the harder question. It’s also the one worth spending the rest of this essay on.

If nothing about this moment is going to correct itself, the only honest next step is to ask whether each of the four levers can be pulled on purpose. Not by waiting for a war to do it, but by someone actually building the conditions.

And here I want to be careful about the language I use, because it matters to how I actually think about this. I’m not interested in punishing anyone or treating wealth as something inherently suspicious. Most of what I’m about to describe isn’t “take something away from the people who have it.” It’s closer to fixing a market that isn’t actually working the way a market is supposed to. A rigged game and a fair one can look identical from a distance. The question is always whether the rules are letting competition do its job.

Take labor bargaining power first, since it’s the lever that did the most work historically. The honest diagnosis isn’t that there’s too much labor relative to demand, full stop. It’s that a lot of real labor markets are quietly monopsonistic. A single large employer, or a small handful of them, ends up setting the wage almost unopposed, not because they’re evil, but because workers face real friction leaving: benefits tied to one job, non-competes that serve no purpose beyond locking someone in place, poor visibility into what the job down the street actually pays. None of that is a healthy, competitive market for labor. It’s a market where one side has been handed an unearned edge by the rules themselves. Loosen those specific frictions, portable benefits, real limits on non-competes that don’t protect an actual trade secret, better wage transparency, and you’re not engineering scarcity out of nowhere. You’re letting ordinary competition do what it’s supposed to do in the first place.

The capital side of this took me longer to land on cleanly, so let me just walk through the actual gap, plainly, because I think the mechanism gets lost if I don’t. If you earn a wage, you’re taxed the moment you earn it. There’s no version of that where you get to defer it for twenty years. But if your wealth is mostly in stock that’s gone up in value, you can borrow against it, live off the loan, and never technically “realize” a gain, which means, under current rules, you never trigger the tax event at all. That’s not a policy someone designed on purpose to favor capital. It’s closer to a seam in the code that happened to open up because one kind of income moves through a bank account and the other kind just sits there, appreciating, untouched, on paper. Closing that specific seam, so capital income faces something closer to the same treatment wage income already faces, isn’t suppressing returns to capital. It’s removing a structural advantage that was never really a deliberate choice to begin with, just an artifact of how the two kinds of income happen to be reported.

The third lever is the one I feel least confident giving you a clean fix for, and I’d rather say that directly than pretend otherwise. The idea is that a lot of what gets called “wealth” is actually a mix of two very different things sitting inside the same number: value someone genuinely created, and value someone managed to extract without creating much of anything. A landlord collecting rent on land that was scarce before they ever touched it isn’t doing the same thing as an engineer who built something that didn’t exist. Right now, both show up identically on a balance sheet, and the tax code, and public opinion, mostly can’t tell them apart. If people could actually see that distinction clearly, source of wealth, not just size of wealth, I think the legitimacy question would sort itself out far better than any tax rate could sort it out for them. But I don’t have a crisp mechanism for how you make that visible at scale, the way I do for the other three. I think this is genuinely the softest of the four.

The fourth lever is, oddly, the one I feel most settled about, maybe because it’s the one that requires the least invention. If concentrated capital ends up captured, it’s often simply because it was allowed to buy direct influence over the very rules meant to constrain it, through lobbying, through financing campaigns, through the ordinary revolving door between industry and regulator. None of that is a mysterious force. It’s a fixable design flaw, in the plainest sense: separate the two things that shouldn’t be touching. Enforce antitrust so no single player gets large enough to write its own rules. Limit how directly money can buy political access. This one barely needs a new idea. It mostly needs the will to actually enforce the ideas that already exist on paper in most functioning democracies.

Put the four side by side and something interesting falls out that I didn’t expect going in. Every single one of them, read this way, is a pro-competition argument, not an anti-wealth one. Fixing a monopsony helps markets work better. Closing a deferral loophole makes two forms of income compete on the same footing instead of one having a private lane. Making value creation visible lets the market itself reward the right thing instead of rewarding whoever’s best at obscuring where their money actually came from. Breaking up capture protects the very rule-of-law conditions a market economy needs to function at all. None of this requires believing rich people are the problem. If anything, it requires believing the opposite, that markets, run honestly, are the thing worth protecting, and that these four fixes are what honest actually looks like in practice.

Which brings me back to where I started. What is actually worth building.

I think the honest answer is that none of this points toward a single clever tool, and I was half hoping it would, because a single tool is a much easier thing to sit down and start writing. What it points toward instead is a much narrower, much less satisfying question: of the four levers, which ones does new technology actually let an individual person move, on their own, without needing a government, a war, or a wealthy benefactor’s permission first.

Three of the four still look, to me, like they need policy, or scale, or both. Labor bargaining power needs portable benefits and enforceable limits on non-competes, which is a legislative fix, not a coding one. The legitimacy piece needs a kind of transparency I still do not have a mechanism for, technological or otherwise. Weakening capture needs campaign finance and antitrust enforcement, which is about political will, not clever architecture.

But the capital side is the one place I keep coming back to, because it might be the first lever in history that a person could plausibly build their way into, rather than only vote or protest their way into. If tools like the ones I started this essay talking about really do let an ordinary person build and own something real, on their own, at a cost that used to require a team and a decade, then for the first time the question of who gets to hold capital is not purely a question of redistribution after the fact. It might, partly, be a question of who is equipped to build in the first place. I do not think that observation solves anything on its own. The same tools that let one person build also concentrate the infrastructure underneath them into fewer and fewer hands, and I am bit unsure which of those two forces wins by default. But it is the one place where the answer is not already written down in a hundred years of failed attempts, and that alone feels like a place worth building toward.

I’m one person figuring this out in public. The more people thinking alongside, the better the thinking gets. Share it if it meant something to you.
