--- title: "Detour: Why I don't feel anxious no more" date: 2026-05-02 subtitle: "What ten years of highs and lows taught me about living without anxiety" ---

Detour, is an experimental series. Same writer, different road: personal essays, lived experiences, and things I haven’t fully figured out yet.

The 16 year old Aayush had lots of expectations from the 25 year old one. Most of them have remained unfulfilled. In the past 10 years, there have rarely been days when I haven’t worked. But it hasn’t been the work you might think. Yes, I did build projects, prepared for exams, studied papers, wrote sometimes — but most of this work wasn’t aimed at finances or academics. It was something else.

My late teens and early twenties were quite chaotic. The few years overlap with covid didn’t help either. Yet something has happened in the past 10 years or so, and that has resulted in me lately feeling a different sense of calmness. It’s been around a year since I left my job to build a startup. For the first time ever in my life, I had complete independence, complete freedom. I have enough balance to care for my utterly low maintenance expenses, I have nobody to report to, and a full creative freedom to take whatever path I want to. And I did kinda leverage it to some extent. I wake up early, go to the gym or boxing sessions, work for most part of the day, and then go out for a walk in the evening in the lively and vibrant streets of HSR. I am loving this life.

But this came with its own set of problems. The biggest of them: uncertainty. I don’t know where I will be next month, which sometimes seems exciting but is dreadful at the same time. I don’t know what I will be doing. When someone asks what I am up to, I don’t know a short answer and the long one seems out of context for a question that is just a conversation starter. Freedom doesn’t mean you don’t have problems, but rather you’re free to choose which ones. And I chose the one I knew I can tackle, because that’s what I spent the last 10 years preparing for.

The reason I am writing this essay is not because I have an answer to the question in title, but I do want to figure it out along with you, the reader, what has happened in the past decade that things have changed so drastically that the feeling I felt the most growing up, seems to have gone away almost entirely.
I have felt the heart sink when certain individuals called out my name, I have felt immensely unenergetic to get out of the bed, I have stared at the fan long enough to see the sun rays striking it in the morning, I have felt the uneasiness in my legs not letting me sleep. I am recalling all of this with a smirk on my face because what felt intimidating that time were actually the first steps to letting that intimidation go. During this course of time I figured out the one thing that was holding me up: fear.

I don’t feel anxious anymore because I fear no more.

Unknowingly, I prepared myself all these years to counter fears every time I faced one. Fear of failure, fear of rejection, fear of being bullied, fear of things not working out, and what not. As cliché as some of these may sound (the “fear of failure” being the most cliché one), they do play important roles in one’s decisions. Most of my decisions, I now realize, weren’t really about getting somewhere. They were about making sure certain fears never got to me twice. I took a drop year after not clearing my engineering entrance exam in the first attempt. Not because I wanted a better college, but just so I could let myself know to not ever settle for the second best. Even if there is the slightest of chances, take it. And it did pay off six years later when I resigned from my job without a hard plan. Not once did I fear about what’s gonna come next.

Sounds like a success story, right? Sorry to break down your expectations… but things didn’t work out as I wanted. Gotcha!

And that happens. Not being afraid doesn’t mean things magically start to go your way. It just means that when things go downhill, you are still composed enough to bear.

Fearlessness stems either from courage or experience. I don’t know if I am courageous enough, I may have to write another essay to figure that out. But I know for certain that the second type is my type. I have had a fair share of good and bad experiences. When you try out a lot of things, with too much passion and compassion, sometimes alone sometimes with other people, you end up with cycles of highs and lows in quick succession. I can’t speak for everyone, but all these cycles in my cases converged to a more “all ends well” in one way or the other. And if they didn’t, I figured out a way. I did not always get what I wanted, actually that happened rarely, but I got what I needed. I figured out the more experiences you have, the more you expose yourself to different situations, the more you become self-aware and that in turn, helps you handle future situations. The confidence that stems from these experiences is what instills the fearlessness.
But here is a caveat. When I set out trying different things and going out of my comfort zone, I failed 9 times out of 10. When you fail 9/10 times, how do you ensure you have sufficient fuel in the tank to go for the 10th shot? If destiny has success written in the 10th attempt, how do you know you won’t buckle on the 9th time?
Every single time I failed, it hurt. And it did break a part of me that wanted the thing in the first place. The next time I tried reaching for it, I realized I wanted it lesser than before. It might have been a coping mechanism to not feel that broken again. Over time, the infatuation with the result faded away.
Exposing yourself to different situations makes you more self-aware. It makes you understand what you truly are beneath the surface. It makes you follow the direction that is most coherent to you. You naturally start walking in that direction. The coherence makes the process more satisfying. Now combine it with the fact that results begin to matter less. What you get in return is a mind that is more inclined towards the process, a mind that now works as a perpetual machine, a mind that doesn’t cloud its decisions with the emotions of the outcome, a mind that is automatically inclined to get better results not because it wants it, but because it follows the right path.

Another thing is: being grateful. And it’s a funny story how I arrived at it. When I first stepped into my college, it was the first time I was away from my home. For the first time in my life, I had eaten a dry roti. See, I grew up in Haryana, which is famous for its food dipped… sorry… drowned in Ghee. And I never realized it until I ate that dry roti. When I went back home after a few months, I ate that Ghee roti again and realized what all I was missing all this time. What I had for 20 years, still never realized it.
When I look at my life so far now, it’s hard to believe how beautiful it’s been. The bad experiences taught me everything that I was missing about the good ones. Which in turn, made me enjoy the good ones even more. The one more reason I can stand nine disappointments in a row is because I get to enjoy the most ordinary moments now.
I am grateful that even after leaving college, I still meet with the people I found there and whom I love so much. One of them is here in Bengaluru, and we often sit out at a tea stall, sometimes even drinking 4-5 cups of chai at once. I enjoy walking down the vibrant streets of HSR. My boxing sessions fill me up with adrenaline in the morning itself, enough to last for the whole day.
Honestly, the low times humbled me. When you are starved of a place, a comfort or a person for a long time, you start noticing exactly when you miss them most. In those moments you live it out in your head — what it would feel like to be there, to be with them. That absence taught me I cannot control everything. The only variable fully in my hands is myself. How I hold up that time. Because I have to. These are cycles after all. When the bad times go, and the good ones come, I know exactly what all I missed and start noticing the small things. I can find happiness in things that I’d have ignored otherwise; as I did to my Ghee roti for 20 years. When you can find satisfactions from the most unreachable corners, the space for anxiety becomes more and more limited. You save enough fuel in the tank to last for all the ten times.

The 16 year old Aayush didn’t have all these experiences that a 25 year old has had. He never knew how it feels to open his heart out, riverside, drinking with his friends. He never knew that once you fail at something, you can still get it. He didn’t know what persistence, consistency and discipline does. He didn’t know heartbreaks make you love deeper, just to a narrower set of people. He didn’t know that missing something or someone at certain times reminds you how you would have enjoyed those moments with them. Prolonged absence humbles you. It trains you to understand that you cannot have what you want, unless you become who deserves it. The 16 year old Aayush had different expectations from the 25 year old one, because he didn’t know these things. But I am sure, the 16 year old Aayush would be proud of where the 25 year old one is headed towards.

I don’t care if the next thing I set out for is gonna be the one, or the one out of nine. It doesn’t matter. The only thing that I do, put my head down and work. Head down, and work. That’s it. I won’t stop working if I do well. I won’t stop working if I don’t. Everything is under my control when I try to control whatever is in my control. The only thing I try to control is today. If it clicks, it clicks. If it doesn’t I’m anyways working tomorrow too, I’ll try again.
