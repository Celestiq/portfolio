--- title: "The law that protects everyone but you" date: 2026-06-20 subtitle: "What happens when the government writes a privacy law and exempts itself from most of it" ---

When Parliament passed the Digital Personal Data Protection Act in 2023, it came wrapped in the language of empowerment. Citizens would finally own their data, and companies that mishandled it would face penalties running up to ₹250 crore. Three years on, corporate India has fallen into line, with boards now fretting over consent flows and breach protocols the way they once fretted over tax.

What almost nobody says aloud is that the same law which holds a small fintech liable for the data of ten thousand users exempts the state, by far the largest collector of personal data in the country, from having to ask your permission to use yours at all.

The drafting was deliberate, and you can see what it permits by looking at what has already happened. Roughly 12.9 crore health accounts under the Ayushman Bharat scheme were created automatically, scraped together from the records of people who had used their Aadhaar to book a Covid vaccine. Most of them found out about their new digital health identity, if they found out at all, from a confirmation SMS. Nobody asked them, and under this law nobody had to.

This is what Section 7 makes possible. Consent is meant to be the heart of the Act, the device through which the law hands you ownership over information about yourself, but Section 7 lays out a long list of “legitimate uses” for which the state needs no fresh consent and owes you no new purpose notice. In a system wired together as tightly as India’s stack, that carve-out runs deep, letting data slide from identity to health to credit to agriculture without anyone stopping to tell you. The law doesn’t really treat your data as yours to guard so much as a kind of public fuel that has to keep flowing for the machinery to work, and in that picture your consent is just friction in the pipes.

If the first move was to free the state from the law, the second was to turn the law into something the state could hide behind. For two decades the Right to Information Act let ordinary people pry open the workings of government, held in check by a public interest test that allowed an official to withhold personal information only by showing that secrecy served some legitimate end. Section 44(3) of the new Act scrapped that test and put a blanket shield in its place, covering anything that “relates to personal information.” The effect showed up almost at once, with officials citing data protection to sit on expenditure records, beneficiary lists and asset declarations, the very documents that let citizens see what their government is up to. A law pitched as a curb on corporate excess has become a wall around the state. The Supreme Court has been hearing constitutional challenges to the provision since early 2026, with petitioners calling it a body blow to open governance, and the fact that the country’s highest court is now picking it apart should put to rest any suggestion that the concern is overblown.

The harder test for any rule, though, is what it does when someone breaks it. Say a government database leaks your data, as the records of hundreds of millions of Indians have, more than once. You file a complaint, and it goes not to a civil court but to the Data Protection Board, whose members the central government appoints and can remove, so you are effectively asking the state’s own nominees to rule against the state. Even if they do rule against it, the penalty is paid into the Consolidated Fund of India, the government’s own account, so the state ends up fining itself and keeping the proceeds. The provision that once let you claim compensation directly was repealed along the way, which means you, the person whose data was exposed, walk away with nothing.

Put the pieces together and the shape is hard to miss. The state doesn’t need your consent to use your data, it can lean on the same law to deny you information about itself, and when it harms you it leaves you pleading before its own appointees for a fine it pays into its own account.

None of this needs bad faith to explain. All it needs is a goal, frictionless digital infrastructure at population scale, and a readiness to treat individual privacy as a cost worth shaving down. The law has done what it was built to do.

The repair work isn’t radical. Section 7 should hold government agencies to the same consent and purpose-limitation standards the Act already demands of private firms. Section 44(3) should go, and the RTI Act’s public interest test should return with it. And the Board’s appointments should bring in members named by the judiciary, so the referee no longer reports to the most powerful player on the pitch. These are corrections rather than reinventions, the least you would need for the law to mean what it claims.

A data protection law stands or falls on a single question. When the most powerful holder of your data fails you, what can you actually do about it? As ours is written, the answer is nothing.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.
