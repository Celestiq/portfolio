--- title: "Call for Social Scientists in the Network Age, Part 2 (A Detour)" date: 2026-03-03 subtitle: "The Floor That Makes Freedom Possible" ---

On a Friday a few days ago, Jack Dorsey posted on X. Block was profitable. Customers were growing. Gross profit was up. None of that mattered. He was cutting 40% of headcount anyway. Over 4,000 people let go because intelligence tools paired with smaller teams had made them unnecessary.

The severance was generous. Twenty weeks of pay. Six months of healthcare. Five thousand dollars for the transition. Jack called the laid-off “temporarily unfortunate, not a permanent underclass.”

Balaji Srinivasan agreed. He posted his own note: “Capitalism is natural selection. Get good or get cut. Work nights and weekends. Become indispensable.” The market is unforgiving, he wrote, because you are the market.

Both men are right about the facts. The automation wave is real. The efficiency gains are real. The displacement is real.

But “temporarily unfortunate” assumes somewhere soft to land. Twenty weeks of pay runs out in week twenty-one. Six months of healthcare ends in month seven.

Who builds that landing?

The Part 2 That Wasn’t

I promised Part 2 would be about who controls the switches between networks. Jack’s note made me write a different one.

Not because the original argument was wrong. Because this one is more urgent. And because, it turns out, they’re the same argument approached from a different direction.

Part 1 said: we connected everyone but didn’t teach them how to coexist. The education system hadn’t caught up with the world people were actually living in. The same failure, one layer down, is happening now. We’re automating everything. Productivity is up. The cost of production is falling. And the systems meant to catch people when they fall (housing, healthcare, public transit, education) are getting more expensive. Not less.

The gains from automation are going somewhere. Just not there.

Get Good. Then What?

Balaji’s argument is seductive because it is not wrong. In a competitive market, the better product wins. The better employee survives. The company that leans into AI outcompetes the one that doesn’t. Jack understood this. He chose the hard, clear cut over the slow, grinding one.

And if you’re the engineer who upskilled in time, this is a fine story. Disruptive, maybe. Navigable.

But the argument has a hidden assumption inside it: that the floor holds. That while you’re retraining, while you’re figuring out what “getting good” means in 2026, the basic costs of staying alive, housed, and healthy are manageable. That the gap between what you earn and what a decent life costs is not so wide that the whole question becomes moot.

In India, that assumption is false. And it’s getting more false every year.

The Gap Nobody Measures

Two numbers matter here. Almost nobody talks about them together.

The minimum wage in Delhi is roughly ₹17,000 to ₹20,000 a month. The legal floor. Designed to prevent extreme exploitation.

The living wage, meaning what a family of four actually needs for a basic but dignified life, is ₹33,125, per Anker Research Institute data for 2025-26. Nearly double.

That gap is widening. Between 2023 and 2026, the living wage in India rose 5 to 8 percent annually. Not because food got expensive. Because healthcare and private schooling did. The things public infrastructure should be providing, and increasingly doesn’t.

Call it the Decency Gap. The distance between the political floor and the real floor. The space where people are technically employed, technically earning, and still unable to afford a life worth living.

Automation compresses the cost of production. It does not compress the cost of a decent life. And if the gains from one don’t go toward closing the other, we will produce more with less and still leave most people behind.

What Income Doesn’t Tell You

Amartya Sen noticed something uncomfortable about how we measure progress: income is the wrong unit.

A thousand rupees in the hands of a disabled person buys different freedoms than a thousand rupees in the hands of someone healthy. A wage increase means nothing if the city has no public buses and your ability to get to work stays at zero. GDP climbs while people’s actual freedom to live, choose, and build stays flat.

Sen called this the Capability Approach. The measure of a good economy is what people are actually free to do, not what they nominally earn.

Martha Nussbaum turned the framework into a concrete list of ten central capabilities a dignified human life requires. The ability to live in good health. To move freely. To think and imagine. To form attachments. To participate in civic life. To play. None of these are luxuries. And all of them depend on infrastructure that exists beneath individual income, or doesn’t.

When Jack’s 4,000 employees collect their twenty weeks of severance, their capability set doesn’t hold steady. Healthcare disappears in month seven. Housing costs don’t pause. Children’s school fees don’t pause. The clock runs. And the generosity of the package matters far less than the solidity of the floor beneath it.

Right now, in most of India, that floor is not solid.

Security Is Not the Enemy of Freedom

The instinct among people who think seriously about markets is to treat public infrastructure as a concession to socialism. A brake. A cost extracted from productivity and handed to those who couldn’t keep up.

That reading has the causality reversed.

A person who doesn’t worry about filling their stomach takes risks. Explores new skills. Starts something. A society of people one medical bill away from catastrophe performs risk-taking while actually just surviving. These look the same from the outside. They produce very different economies.

The automation transition needs genuine explorers. People who, freed from survival mode, can figure out what the new economy requires and build toward it. A guaranteed floor produces exactly the restless, curious, self-directed people Balaji is asking employees to become. Survival mode produces compliance. Security produces agency.

Investing automation’s gains into closing the Decency Gap is the precondition for capitalism’s next phase to function, at scale and for everyone. A deeper reading of what markets need, not a retreat from them.

Where the Gains Should Go

The productivity gains from automation are real. The question of where they go is not being asked loudly enough.

GDP will look fine. Production is up. Efficiency is up. The number will glow. But the living wage is climbing. The Decency Gap is widening. And the people Jack called “temporarily unfortunate” will find that temporary is a generous word when the floor keeps moving.

The infrastructure phase of the internet is over. We’re now in the transition phase of automation. And we’re making the same mistake we made with connectivity: assuming the gains will distribute themselves, that the floor will hold, that individuals just need to get good fast enough.

They won’t. It won’t. They can’t, not without somewhere to stand.

Education is Part 1’s answer. It scales. It works. But education requires a floor beneath it. A person cannot learn to navigate a networked society while wondering how to pay for their child’s antibiotics. The arguments are not in competition. They are sequential. First the floor. Then the map.

The gains from automation should build that floor. Housing that doesn’t consume half a salary. Healthcare that doesn’t bankrupt a family. Public transit that makes geography less deterministic. Education whose cost doesn’t compound the Decency Gap year after year.

The basic capabilities that make freedom real, not just theoretical.

That is a deeper reading of what markets need to work. And it starts with asking the question Jack didn’t ask: not “how do we cushion the disruption?” but “what does the landing need to look like?”

Balaji told the 4,000 to get good.
He’s right that they should.
But getting good requires somewhere to stand while you do it.

Building that floor is the investment that makes the next economy possible. The one where people are free enough from survival to explore, build, and take the risks that markets need them to take.

The infrastructure phase is over.
The coexistence phase has begun.
This time, we know exactly what we’re missing.
