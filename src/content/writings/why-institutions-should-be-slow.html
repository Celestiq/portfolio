--- title: "Why Institutions Should Be Slow" date: 2026-04-16 subtitle: "The frustration with slow institutions is legitimate. The conclusion people draw from it isn't." ---

In late 2015, Facebook had billboards up across India, a partnership with Reliance signed, and Free Basics already live. A billion Indians didn’t have internet access, and the service would get them online for free. TRAI, meanwhile, was running a consultation, releasing papers, collecting responses, taking its time, while a trillion-dollar company reshaped what connectivity meant for first-time internet users. The frustration with the regulator was understandable, because every month of delay was another month of Facebook defining what the internet was for people who had no other frame of reference.

What holding actually requires

The instinct toward speed skips a question worth sitting with. What do we actually want from an institution when it acts? A decision can move fast and still not hold, because holding requires that the people who have to live under it had some encounter with the question before the answer arrived.

Most rules don’t hold because of enforcement; they hold because people have come to believe them as theirs, and that process requires the question to be genuinely public before it closes. A decision made quickly, by people responding to whoever showed up loudest, produces a technically valid answer that sits in a society without roots.

The lag was also diagnosis

The TRAI consultation drew over a million responses, a large chunk being template emails from Facebook’s own campaign, which TRAI publicly discounted. What remained came from founders who’d mapped the competitive architecture underneath Free Basics’ charity pitch, and activists who understood what a walled garden does to users who’ve never known another internet. The process was slow because it was genuinely open, and that openness made the question visible before anyone had to live with the answer.

Slowness also gathers information. In mid-2015, what Free Basics would actually do to the internet wasn’t fully visible, and the pitch worked precisely because of that gap. What months of public argument surfaced was the mechanism underneath: that first-time users would have their mental model of the internet shaped entirely by what Facebook chose to include, that reaching those users as a competitor required Facebook’s approval, that “free access” was a distribution monopoly wearing the clothes of inclusion. A faster decision would’ve been made before any of that came into view.

A decision made quickly, by people responding to whoever showed up loudest, produces a technically valid answer that sits in a society without roots.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.

The wrong baseline

TRAI’s February 2016 ruling banned differential data pricing entirely, and over the four years that followed, TDSAT upheld it and the Supreme Court backed net neutrality as a foundational principle. A decision built through a slow, messy, public process absorbed every legal challenge without bending.

The frustration in 2015 was measuring against the wrong baseline. When an institution slows down, the vacuum doesn’t stay empty, and in this case it was Facebook ready to fill it, moving fast because speed served Facebook. Regulatory lag gets called failure, but what the lag was doing here was turning a question that could’ve been settled in private into one that had to be worked out in public, with enough of a record underneath it that no court could simply wave it away.

I’m one person figuring this out in public. The more people thinking alongside, the better the thinking gets. Share it if it meant something to you.
