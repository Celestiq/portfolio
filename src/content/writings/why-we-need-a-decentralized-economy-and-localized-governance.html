--- title: "Why We Need a Decentralized Economy and Localized Governance" date: 2026-06-10 subtitle: "On the ideas behind what I'm building" ---

The software running most of the internet was not built by a company. Linux sits underneath the majority of the world’s servers, inside billions of phones, behind the systems that move money between banks, and it was assembled over three decades by people who mostly never met, answering to no executive, paid by no one for most of the work. Wikipedia outlasted the encyclopedias it replaced on the labour of volunteers. The protocols carrying this sentence to your screen were worked out in committees that no government owned and no shareholder could direct.

We tend to file this away as a curiosity of the digital age, a happy accident of clever people and cheap bandwidth. I’ve come to think it’s something much larger. It might be the clearest evidence we have that enormously complex, critical, widely trusted systems can be built and maintained without anyone being in charge. And once you take that seriously, it becomes hard to ignore the obvious follow-up, which is that if this works here, and works better than the centralized alternative, almost nothing else in our lives is organized anything like it.

The answer is less flattering than I wanted it to be. We don’t run our economic and political life through distributed self-governance because the world has been gradually rebuilt to make that look impossible, and we mistake the result of that rebuilding for the natural order of things.

Think about the suburb that grew up around a highway. The highway came first, a reasonable decision in its moment, and development followed the road until businesses had clustered along it and the old town centre, where people once walked to what they needed, lost the commercial base that held it together. The density that might have supported a tram or a market never formed. Now the place simply cannot function without cars, not because anyone ever chose car-dependence as a goal, but because each sensible step along the way reshaped the ground a little more, until walking became unimaginable and driving became the only life on offer. The infrastructure remade the town in its own image, and the remade town kept no memory of any other way to live.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.

This is the pattern centralized systems follow at scale, and it has nothing to do with anyone’s intentions. Roman roads made Roman administration feel necessary in the same way the national grid later made centralized generation feel like the only serious option, and global logistics made local productive capacity look quaint, even a little embarrassing. In each case the system rearranged the world around its own requirements, narrowing what counted as realistic, until the alternatives looked like nostalgia. So when someone today says a decentralized economy is utopian, they’re half right, but the impossibility they’re pointing at was manufactured. We inherited a world already built to make the current arrangement seem like the only one that could exist.

If the trap were only that, you could imagine reforming your way out of it, one careful adjustment at a time. What makes the situation genuinely hard is the second thing I kept running into, which is that centralized systems can’t correct themselves fast enough to escape their own mistakes.

I watched this closely during the first months of the pandemic. Some communities moved in days, with mutual aid networks forming, local businesses changing what they made, neighbours inventing workable rules for shared spaces before any official guidance existed. Others waited, because waiting was the structure they lived inside. They waited for the centre to decide what the situation even was, and by the time instructions came down, the thing those instructions described had already changed underneath them, so that the official response kept arriving a few weeks too late to be the response anyone actually needed.

That gap is built into the architecture. Information has to climb up through administrative layers, get processed somewhere far away, turn into policy, and travel back down. In a slow world you can live with the delay. In a world where a neighbourhood’s labour market can be reshaped by a single technology in a year, where the facts on the ground shift faster than zoning laws can be amended, the delay stops being an inconvenience and becomes a kind of blindness. A system survives change by changing itself faster than the change arrives. Local governance is quicker for an unglamorous reason, which is that the people living through the change and the people authorised to respond to it are the same people. Nothing has to be transmitted upward, nothing gets lost in translation, and nobody has to wait for a distant office to grasp what’s already obvious to everyone standing in the street.

By this point in my own thinking I wanted to jump to solutions, and I had to stop myself, because the easy version of the solution is wrong. The easy version says push power downward, hand communities their authority back, and the problem dissolves. It doesn’t, and working out why is what took me to the harder part of the argument.

Let’s picture three people. The first has every political freedom you could name, she votes, she speaks, she organises, but her livelihood depends entirely on a firm that could restructure overnight and pull the economic floor out from under her whole city. The second has total economic independence, land and tools and the means to feed herself, but she lives under rules she had no hand in shaping, rules that change without her knowledge and are enforced by people she’ll never meet. The third has neither. We look at these as three different problems needing three different fixes, and we fail at all three, because the thing missing in every case is the same. The person who knows, the person who decides, and the person who lives with the result have been pulled apart from one another. Political freedom without economic independence is a performance. Economic independence without a say in the rules is a cage with a comfortable interior. And both of them, stripped of any real community, of the web of mutual need that makes freedom worth having and independence worth using, are just private conditions with nowhere to go. Sovereignty isn’t one thing you can win. It’s layered, and broken at any layer it fails at all of them.

So the rebuilding has to happen at every layer at once, which sounds exhausting until you notice that the layers feed each other. And this is where the essay arrived somewhere I didn’t expect when I started writing it.

Take two cities that install the exact same thing, direct resident voting on how the local budget gets spent. In one, it withers. Turnout is thin, organised interests capture the process, and the whole exercise becomes theatre that ratifies decisions made elsewhere. In the other, it comes alive. People argue well, hold each other accountable, surprise themselves with what they come up with. Same mechanism, opposite outcomes, and the difference isn’t intelligence or some special civic virtue in the water. It’s habit. The first city is full of residents, people who receive services, pay their taxes, and expect governance to happen to them. The second is full of citizens, people who understand themselves as the authors of their shared life rather than its subjects.

You cannot bolt genuine self-governance onto a population that a century of centralized economy and representative politics has trained into passivity, and expect it to hold. Not because those people are deficient, but because the human being capable of governing herself is produced by the practice of governing herself, and that practice has been steadily replaced by the act of consuming. The structure and the human being who can inhabit it aren’t two separate projects you tackle in sequence. They’re the same project, approached from two directions, each one making the other possible.

Which brings me back to the volunteers who built the systems the rest of us now lean on every day. What looked at the start of this like a curiosity of the internet reads differently now. Those communities work because the people who hold the knowledge are the same people who make the decisions, and the people who make the decisions are the same people who live with what they’ve built. The loop is closed. The knowledge and the authority sit in the same hands. The collaboration runs on something real rather than manufactured dependence, which is why nobody has to be in charge for it to function.

That, finally, is the thing underneath everything I’ve been circling. A society of genuinely self-sovereign people who choose to depend on one another doesn’t need to be governed into good outcomes from above. It produces them, the way a healthy body maintains itself, because every part of it can sense and answer to every other part without permission from a centre. Decentralized economy and localized governance aren’t policies you adopt. They’re what becomes possible once people stop being subjects of a system and start being authors of a shared life.

I’m one person figuring this out in public. The more people thinking alongside, the better the thinking gets. Share it if it meant something to you.
