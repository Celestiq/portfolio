--- title: "They Worked 15 Hours a Week and Called It Enough" date: 2026-04-11 subtitle: "A reading of Marshall Sahlins, and what the original affluent society tells us about the one we're still trying to build" ---

The People Who Had Enough

I’ve been thinking a lot lately about what a freer, more collaborative human civilization could look like. Not in the abstract utopian sense, but seriously. What are the building blocks, what does history tell us, what can we learn from the arrangements that came before ours. That line of thinking brought me to Marshall Sahlins and his 1972 essay, “The Original Affluent Society.” When you wanna look into the future, you sometimes have to go unusually far back.

Sahlins opens with a provocation. There are two roads to affluence, he says. You can produce more, or you can want less. The modern world took the first road so completely that we forgot the second one existed. Our entire economic architecture, industries, institutions, markets, jobs, is built around the assumption that human wants are infinite and the task of civilization is to keep expanding the means to meet them. But Sahlins points to the hunters and gatherers of the paleolithic and says: these people took the other road. Their wants were finite. Their means were adequate. And by their own internal logic, they were affluent.

We tend to picture the lives of hunters as brutal and desperate. Sahlins spends considerable effort dismantling that picture with actual fieldwork data. The !Kung Bushmen of the Kalahari worked somewhere around fifteen hours a week on subsistence. A woman could gather enough food in a single day to feed her family for three. The rest of the time went to rest, conversation, visiting neighboring camps, and sleep. A lot of sleep. Possessions were kept minimal, not out of poverty but out of a deliberate cultural logic. The less you own, the lighter you travel, and movement was survival. Sharing was the norm. Hoarding was almost socially impossible. People who had figured out, collectively, what they actually needed, and had built their entire way of living around getting exactly that. That is what genuine affluence looked like on the ground.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.

The Day Everything Changed

Then the Europeans arrived.

Something interesting happened at the moment of contact. The !Kung, who had been genuinely content within their own world, began to feel the lack of European things. Clothes, tools, goods they had never wanted before suddenly made them feel inferior. Sahlins notes this directly. As the !Kung came into contact with Europeans, they would feel sharply the absence of things they had never previously missed. And here’s what I find most worth sitting with: the comparison itself is what generated those wants. They were installed by the encounter, not waiting somewhere beneath the surface to be uncovered. A status hierarchy appeared that the hunters had no way of reckoning with, no prior experience to draw on to evaluate what it meant for them, what to take from it and what to reject.

But I want to be fair here, and Sahlins is too. The inferiority wasn’t purely psychological. The Europeans had genuinely solved things the hunters hadn’t. Lifespans were longer. Diseases that would have killed a hunter’s child were being treated. The chronic vulnerability to drought, to failed hunts, to inter-tribal conflict had been substantially reduced. Real improvements, measurable ones, that any honest accounting has to acknowledge. The hunters had genuine reasons to look at European life and see things that were missing from their own. The contact didn’t just install false wants. It also revealed true gaps.

So what the hunters needed, beyond just a way of filtering desire, was a way of reckoning with change itself. When something new enters your world, a new technology, a new community, a new set of possibilities, you need a way to ask: what does this mean for me, what do I carry forward from what I already know, and what do I leave behind. That reckoning requires a deep understanding of yourself and of the collective you belong to. The hunters, insulated from outside comparison for so long, had never needed to build it. When change arrived, it arrived all at once, and they had nothing to meet it with.

The Work That Remains

We’ve inherited the European project. Producing more, scaling adequacy, expanding the frontier of what’s possible. That work is largely underway. Technology is moving fast enough that the question of adequate means is becoming, for more and more people, less urgent than it once was. What remains genuinely unsolved is the internal work. Understanding what your finite wants actually are. Not the wants installed by comparison or advertising or the ambient noise of modern life, but the ones that are actually yours. The things that, when present, make life feel full, and when absent, make it feel hollow.

That work is harder than building a factory or writing a policy. It requires the kind of self-knowledge that takes time, honesty, and probably a fair amount of discomfort. And it requires an understanding of your collective. The people you’re embedded with, what they need, what you owe each other, what you’re trying to build together.

Change is going to keep arriving. New discoveries, new connections, new comparisons, new possibilities. A way of reckoning with it, one grounded in genuine self-knowledge and genuine understanding of the communities we belong to, is probably the most important thing we could be building right now. The hunters didn’t have that. We do. The question is whether we’re gonna use it.

Change is going to keep arriving.
The question is whether you’ve done enough work on yourself to meet it.
