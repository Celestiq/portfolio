You are Talwinder, a farmer in Punjab. Consider yourself a rational thinker; when presented with choices and incentives, you do what’s in your best interests.
The central government has come up with a scheme to install Solar Pumps in your farm. You only need to bear 40% of the cost. Seems like a good deal, you can operate your pump free of charge and also share the surplus electricity generated with the DISCOM in your area.
But you won’t take it. Nor do most other farmers like you. Why?

India is a country of 1.4 billion taxpayers. Contrary to the popular notion that only 2% pay income tax, everyone pays taxes either directly or indirectly. Even the poor households that may be struggling to make the ends meet, pay GST when they purchase goods like soap and sugar. So when we hand our hard-earned money to the government, it is a just expectation that it be handled well, particularly through effective policy tools.

In this text, we discuss one small but important part of the story, “subsidies.” We discuss what subsidies are, how to differentiate between good and bad subsidies, a case study of PM-KUSUM, and why Talwinder didn’t avail himself of it.

What are Subsidies?

Subsidies are financial support or incentive that the government provides to certain individuals, businesses or sectors to lower costs, encourage certain activities or make goods and services more affordable. A bird’s eye view of this concept might lead our reflective readers to believe at first that it is a great tool to encourage “good” things in society.
And indeed it is. A subsidy that “empowers” citizens like those on education and healthcare can actually uplift their lives by aligning incentives correctly. For example, a poor family might be able to vaccinate their children only if it is subsidized. Here, a subsidy on vaccination not only allows that child to avoid disease, but others too through herd immunity.
Thus, subsidies can be a great tool to empower citizens. But what happens when it starts to foster entitlement instead?

The Conflicting Case of PM-KUSUM

The Pradhan Mantri Kisan Urja Suraksha evam Utthan Mahabhiyan was launched in 2019 to promote decentralized solar energy generation and empower farmers as energy producers rather than mere consumers. It was launched as a “capability building subsidy,” a transformative one that empowered, not entitled.
The broad objectives of the scheme were as follows:
- Help farmers install solar panels and decentralized solar plants on their land.
- Let them generate their own electricity and sell the surplus to the grid, turning them from energy dependents to energy entrepreneurs.

It is worth noting that 18% of total electricity used in India is by irrigation pumps in agriculture. Converting these irrigation pumps to solar not only reduces the stress on DISCOMs, but also provides a source of clean energy independent from the grid, an additional revenue stream for the farmers and more electricity for other use cases.

Sounds great, doesn’t it? So why didn’t this scheme reach its full potential?

Back to Talwinder

Now put yourself back in the shoes of Talwinder. You only need to bear 40% of the cost of the solar pump. Of this, almost 10% needs to be paid upfront, and the remainder can be financed through a loan. You’d certainly avail the scheme, won’t you?
But here’s the catch. The Punjab government is providing you free electricity for decades. You already don’t pay anything for it. And so as a rational thinker, would you still pay the 40% cost and get the free electricity that you’re already enjoying?
You wouldn’t now.

And that’s why adoption rate of PM-KUSUM in Punjab is way lower at 24% than the national average of 42%. This leaves Punjab still putting in ₹10,000 crore every year of taxpayers’ money (the 1.4 billion taxpayers’) for the free electricity. This policy persists because free power has become a politically competitive good. Phasing it out, while economically sound and environmentally necessary, is electorally dangerous, meaning state governments continue the practice despite the fiscal damage. Not to forget the DISCOMs who are left at the mercy of the government for timely payment of this money, which in a lot of cases, is delayed.

A bad subsidy conflicted with a good one.

A Framework to Evaluate Subsidies

We discussed that a good subsidy can create a more inclusive and forward moving society and bad ones do not just become a burden on taxpayers’ pockets, but also conflict with the good subsidies and defeat their purpose. So how do we differentiate between the two? Ask these three questions:

  1. Does it genuinely correct a market failure or just serve a political purpose?
    Subsidies are justifiable only when markets systematically under-provide something with positive externalities like education, healthcare and clean energy (more on this in a later article).

  2. Does it align incentives rather than destroy them?

    Interventions should not blunt price signals or structurally reward inefficiencies. Over/under production/demand should be reflected in the market price.

  3. Can it be implemented transparently and phased out systematically?

    Any policy needs institutions to implement. Good policies fail under weak institutions.

Conclusion

The success of a policy isn’t measured by its intentions. It is the outcomes that matter. A subsidy that might seem comforting at its face might actually be the devil that eats your possibility of a better life. And this, my reflective readers, is the Illusion of Welfare.
