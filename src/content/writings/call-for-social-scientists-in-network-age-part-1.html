--- title: "Call for Social Scientists in Network Age - Part 1" date: 2026-02-10 subtitle: "We Connected Everyone. Now We Need to Teach Them How to Coexist." ---

Three stories from February 2026 reveal what happens when technical skill exists without social understanding.

Coimbatore, February 6th. A 20-year-old engineering student collects photos of 24 female classmates from social media and WhatsApp groups, uses “one-click” AI tools to morph them into semi-nude images, and posts them from a fake Instagram account. He assumes anonymity will protect him.

It doesn’t. He’s arrested within days.

The student had technical proficiency. He knew how to prompt AI, navigate digital tools, bypass basic security; all the skills we celebrate in modern education. But he had zero understanding of consent in networked spaces, zero grasp of digital permanence, zero awareness of IT Act Section 67A. He treated human beings as content for a prank, failing to understand that digital actions have permanent, physical-world consequences.

Ghaziabad, February 4th. Three sisters (aged 12, 14, and 16) jump from the ninth floor of their building following a dispute with their parents over addiction to a Korean task-based game. They’d withdrawn from school for three years, retreating into a fantasy world where the game’s escalating psychological tasks replaced real relationships.

Notes found in their room: crushing loneliness. A distorted reality where “death was better than beatings.” The beatings being parental restrictions on phone access.

They were trapped in an algorithmic reward loop designed to replace real-world authority with platform validation, a system that exploited the same psychological vulnerabilities that make slot machines addictive. Their education never gave them frameworks for emotional regulation in digital spaces. Never taught them to distinguish between algorithmic connection and human connection.

Lucknow, late January. A man arguing with authorities gets recorded. The video is clipped, stripped of context, uploaded. Within hours: “anti-national.” His address gets doxxed. Hundreds pile on. Educated professionals, each seeing themselves as individuals responding to a video. Not a mob.

The platforms amplify it. Engagement equals profit. By the time context emerges, the damage is permanent.

These aren’t stupid people.

The engineering student had technical literacy. The three sisters were navigating sophisticated gaming systems with interfaces more complex than most professional software. The people piling on in that video included professionals who’d passed their digital literacy courses, who knew to check sources and avoid phishing, who considered themselves informed citizens.

But none of them had frameworks to understand what they were doing. Or what was being done to them.

1. The friction moved, but the education didn’t

For three decades, success meant connectivity. Bandwidth. Users online. Latency reduction. The metrics were clean, the philosophy simple: connect everyone, and good things will happen.

By the early 2020s, mission was accomplished. The pipes were laid. Approximately 50% of the developing world got connected in just two decades, faster than any infrastructure in history, faster than electricity or railways or running water had ever spread.

But the friction moved.

Technical problems (packet loss, routing errors, hardware limitations) gave way to social problems: the collision of communities, values, contexts. The seamless transmission of data created the turbulent collision of humans.

Throughout history, geography acted as a natural buffer. Different communities could hold contradictory worldviews without constant conflict. Physical distance created cool-down periods, local norms evolved for bounded spaces and disputes had time and space to breathe.

Digital networks collapse this distance by design.

They force everyone, with incompatible communication styles, moral frameworks, epistemologies, into a single shared space where your grandmother and your boss and your college friends and hostile strangers all witness the same performance simultaneously. There’s no cool-down period. And the algorithms amplify whatever generates engagement, which turns out to be outrage, which turns out to be profitable.

This is “context collapse.” danah boyd identified it years ago. We’re still pretending it doesn’t exist.

The three stories reveal three different gaps:

Ethics vs. Skill (Coimbatore). Technical literacy without ethical frameworks. The student knew how to use AI tools but not what it means to use them on real people, because nobody taught him that technical capability doesn’t grant moral permission. He had capability without consequence-awareness.

Reel vs. Real (Ghaziabad). Gaming literacy without frameworks for understanding platform mechanics. The sisters couldn’t distinguish between algorithmic validation and human connection, between a reward system designed by psychologists to maximize engagement and genuine relationships that require vulnerability and presence. They had engagement without understanding what was engaging them.

Individual vs. Mob (Lucknow). Digital access without understanding of network dynamics. Each person saw themselves as responding individually to a clear case of wrongdoing, not recognizing that their individual action was being aggregated by algorithms into collective violence. They had agency without awareness of emergence.

If everyone now lives in networked society, why are we educating people as if they still live in isolated villages?

2. We teach people to drive without teaching traffic laws

Current digital literacy: Use strong passwords. Check your sources. Don’t click suspicious links. Know how to use Word and Excel. Maybe learn to code.

All necessary. All insufficient.

Here’s what we don’t teach:

Network dynamics and emergence. How individual actions create unpredictable collective outcomes. Why “one share” or “one comment” can fundamentally change everything. Emergence: how simple rules (like, share, retweet) create complex mob behavior no one intended.

The people piling on that video needed this. Each person thought they were acting individually, making an independent moral judgment about clear wrongdoing. None understood they were part of an emergent phenomenon: a digital pile-on where the whole is catastrophically greater than the sum of its parts, where individual acts of righteousness aggregate into collective violence. Network amplification isn’t additive. It’s exponential. And most people have no framework for understanding exponential dynamics.

Context collapse and the invisible audience. Why the same content reads completely differently to different audiences. The impossibility of controlling narrative once content leaves your hands. Why “deleting” or “going private” often makes things worse. How video clips stripped of context become weapons.

All three stories needed this. The engineering student didn’t understand his “prank” would be seen by parents, police, courts, and the public. He thought he was performing for an invisible audience of peers who would find it funny. The viral video jumped contexts (from local dispute to national outrage) and nobody understood how or why, because the medium itself creates this jump through algorithmic amplification and decontextualization.

Platform mechanics and the attention economy. What metrics platforms optimize for. (Spoiler: not your wellbeing.) How recommendation algorithms shape what you see and amplify division. Why false or emotionally charged content spreads faster than truth: novelty plus emotional arousal equals virality. The reward loops in games and social media designed to be more compelling than reality.

The three sisters needed this. They were trapped in a system scientifically engineered to hijack their reward circuitry through variable ratio reinforcement schedules (the same mechanism that makes slot machines addictive), where the game’s unpredictable rewards triggered dopamine releases stronger than anything homework or family dinners could provide. Without frameworks to understand this, their parents could only see “addiction” and respond with restrictions. Which escalated the crisis. Because you can’t solve a systems problem with willpower.

Consent, permanence, and digital ethics. Understanding digital content as permanent public record, not ephemeral conversation. Consent in the age of screenshots, deepfakes, and viral spread. The legal and ethical stakes of digital harm (it’s not “just online”). How AI tools amplify the scale of harm. One click becomes 24 victims.

The 20-year-old with the AI tools needed this. He had the technical skill to generate deepfakes but no framework for understanding he was committing sexual harassment at scale, that each morphed image was a separate violation, that “anonymity” on the internet is a fiction maintained only until someone decides to pierce it. He thought anonymity meant impunity. It doesn’t. Technical literacy without ethical literacy is dangerous. Actually dangerous.

We’re teaching people to drive without teaching traffic laws. We’re teaching literacy without teaching rhetoric: how to read, but not how persuasion, manipulation, and mob dynamics work in networked spaces.

People aren’t stupid. They’re navigating without maps.

And the consequences are catastrophic.

Less hype, more thinking. On technology, policy, and the world they quietly shape.

3. When reward loops replace reality, parents don’t have the language to intervene

The tragedy of those three sisters isn’t isolated.

Young people globally are forming identity in networked spaces where validation is algorithmic. Experiencing industrialized social comparison (seeing curated highlight reels of thousands). Managing fragmented attention where notifications destroy the ability to focus or be present. Living in environments where digital life feels more real than physical life, where the dopamine hit from a like outweighs the satisfaction of a conversation, where online drama feels more urgent than offline relationships.

The academic debate: Is social media causing a mental health crisis?

Jonathan Haidt says yes. The “Great Rewiring” between 2010-2015 (smartphone plus social media adoption) correlates with a synchronized spike in teen anxiety and depression across the Anglosphere, a pattern too consistent across countries and demographics to be coincidental. 134% increase in hospitalizations for depression among teen girls. The mechanisms: comparison, attention fragmentation, sleep disruption.

Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski say the effect is small. 0.4% of variance in adolescent wellbeing. Comparable to eating potatoes or wearing glasses. “Screen time” is too blunt a metric. Need to look at what people do, not duration. Risk of moral panic.

But here’s what both sides miss: Whether the effect is large or small, design matters and understanding helps.

The parents in Ghaziabad saw “addiction.” They responded with restrictions. Power struggle ensued. Escalation. Tragedy.

They didn’t have the language for “variable ratio reinforcement schedule.” Couldn’t explain why the game was designed to be more compelling than homework, friends, family, life itself; why professional psychologists had calibrated every reward, every level, every social pressure to maximize engagement above all else. Couldn’t name “parasocial relationships” or “algorithmic identity formation.” Couldn’t distinguish between their daughters’ genuine preferences and preferences shaped by systems optimizing for engagement, not wellbeing.

Without frameworks, they were reduced to confiscating phones and hoping for the best.

That’s not a parenting failure. That’s an education system failure.

What understanding provides:

Recognition. Not “I feel terrible after scrolling” but “I’m experiencing algorithmically-driven social comparison designed to keep me engaged.”

Agency. Informed choices rather than shame-based abstinence or parent-child power struggles. You can’t make good decisions about systems you don’t understand.

Resilience. Understanding doesn’t equal immunity. But it transforms helplessness into agency. You can’t defend against manipulation you can’t name.

Language. Parents and children can talk about “reward loops” and “context collapse” instead of just fighting about “screen time.” Shared vocabulary enables shared navigation.

This applies beyond teens. Adults experience this too. The hundreds who piled on that video didn’t recognize they were being manipulated into mob behavior. The student with the deepfake tools didn’t recognize he was committing a crime that would follow him forever. Everyone managing reputation across contexts without frameworks for understanding context itself.

Understanding these dynamics isn’t optional luxury. It’s basic survival literacy in networked society.

4. The curriculum exists in the wrong places

The knowledge exists. Network effects. Context collapse. Algorithmic amplification. Reward loops. Emergent behavior. Surveillance capitalism. Digital ethics.

It’s just locked in tech companies and academia.

Tech companies hire sociologists, psychologists, political scientists to understand and manage these dynamics because they need this knowledge to build platforms that don’t immediately collapse into chaos (or more cynically, to build platforms that do collapse into chaos in profitable ways, that maximize engagement even at the cost of wellbeing). They pay six-figure salaries for expertise that should be basic literacy.

Academia studies these phenomena rigorously. Computational social science. Digital sociology. Platform studies. The research exists in peer-reviewed journals that nobody outside the field reads.

But it doesn’t reach the people who need it most: everyone living in networked society.

The curriculum update isn’t an addition. It’s updating existing subjects for the reality students already inhabit.

Civics becomes: How does democracy work when mobs form in hours? What is consent in networked spaces? How do platforms mediate political discourse?

Psychology includes: Digital wellbeing. Reward loops and habit formation. Social comparison theory. Identity formation in networked environments. Emotional regulation across contexts.

Sociology covers: Network effects and power laws. Digital tribes and affective polarization. Context collapse. Mob dynamics. The Space of Flows versus the Space of Places.

Computer Science isn’t just coding. It’s understanding algorithmic systems and their social effects. What does a recommendation engine optimize for? How does that shape information environments?

Ethics addresses: Digital harm is real harm. Consent and permanence. Scale of impact. The ethics of AI-generated content. Responsibility in networked spaces.

The concepts:

  • Network effects and emergence (individual actions → collective behavior)

  • Context collapse and the invisible audience

  • Algorithmic amplification and recommendation systems

  • Reward loops and attention economy mechanics

  • Cognitive biases (novelty bias, confirmation bias, social comparison)

  • Digital ethics: consent, permanence, scale

The skills:

  • Recognizing when you’re part of an algorithmically-amplified mob

  • Understanding platform incentives (what does this system optimize for?)

  • Managing digital communication without body language or context

  • Distinguishing between algorithmic validation and human connection

  • Building healthy boundaries with platforms designed to be addictive

Age-appropriate progression from elementary through high school. Not dumbed down. Updated.

5. Three stories, one education gap

The engineering student needed ethics education before he had access to AI tools. Understanding consent. Understanding permanence. Understanding that technical capability doesn’t grant moral permission, that being able to do something doesn’t mean you should, that anonymity is a procedural barrier not a moral shield.

The three sisters needed frameworks to understand what was happening to them. Recognition that the game was designed to exploit their psychology through mechanisms refined over decades of behavioral research. Language to communicate their experience beyond “I can’t stop playing.” Parents who could engage with the actual problem instead of just removing the phone, who understood they were fighting a system engineered by professionals, not their daughters’ weakness.

The people in that mob needed to recognize mob dynamics before they became one. Understanding that individual actions aggregate into collective violence through algorithmic amplification that treats outrage as engagement and engagement as profit. That context collapse turns local disputes into national outrages by stripping away nuance and amplifying emotion. That platforms amplify division because engagement equals profit, and nothing engages like righteous anger.

We built a new world. One where distance has collapsed. Where algorithms amplify our worst impulses. Where technical skills without social understanding become weapons.

Now we need to teach people how to live in it.

Not how to escape it. How to thrive in it with understanding, agency, and dignity.

The next tragedy is already forming. Parents are already fighting with children about phones without frameworks to understand what’s actually happening, treating systemic design problems as individual willpower failures. Students are already acquiring powerful AI tools without ethics education, learning capability without responsibility. Mobs are already forming around decontextualized clips, individual righteousness aggregating into collective violence through platforms that profit from the chaos.

Education is the only intervention that scales.

The infrastructure phase is over. The coexistence phase has begun. And we’re sending people into it without maps, without frameworks, without the most basic literacy for navigating networked society, like teaching someone to swim by throwing them in the ocean and hoping they figure it out before they drown.

That’s not a technology problem. That’s our problem. And we can fix it.

If we recognize it exists.
