--- title: "History Already Knows How the Internet Chaos Ends" date: 2026-04-14 subtitle: "The printing press did it first. What Europe built in response took 150 years. We are somewhere near the beginning of ours." ---

Before the Enlightenment, There Was Fire

The story most of us were told goes something like this: Gutenberg invented the printing press in the 1440s, knowledge spread, the Church’s monopoly on truth broke, and humanity walked steadily into the Enlightenment. Reason won. Science flourished. The modern world was born.

It’s a clean story, except it’s missing roughly 150 years of catastrophe that almost nobody talks about.

The printing press didn’t cause the Enlightenment directly... what it caused first was the collapse of shared reality across an entire civilization, and the wars that followed when people could no longer agree on what was true.

Before the Press: A World Held Together by Friction

To understand why, you’ve gotta understand what the world looked like before Gutenberg.

In medieval Europe, information moved slowly and at extraordinary expense. Monks copied manuscripts by hand, one painstaking page at a time. Only texts deemed valuable by ecclesiastical or academic elites were ever reproduced at scale. This was, in a sense, a filter. A brutal and often unjust one, it kept the powerful powerful and the ignorant ignorant, but a filter nonetheless. Heterodox ideas existed, but they died quietly, contained within small communities who could never reach a mass audience. The Catholic Church held a near-monopoly on what counted as truth, enforced not by argument but by the sheer friction of information itself.

The press destroyed that friction overnight. Suddenly, anyone with basic capital could print anything: theological arguments, political pamphlets, outright fabrications, and every page would carry the exact same typographic authority as centuries of Church doctrine. The medium conferred legitimacy regardless of the message.

The First Media Personality in History

Martin Luther, a German monk with sharp theological instincts and an even sharper grasp of communication, was perhaps the first person to truly understand what the press meant for power.

He wasn’t the first to challenge the Catholic Church; earlier reformers like Jan Hus in Bohemia had made remarkably similar theological arguments a century before, questioning papal authority, condemning institutional corruption, advocating for scripture in the common tongue. Hus was burned at the stake, and his ideas died with him because there was no mechanism to spread them faster than the Church could suppress them.

Luther had a printing press.

When he published his 95 Theses in 1517, a direct challenge to the Church’s practice of selling “indulgences” (essentially paid pardons for sin), he didn’t send them to Rome for debate. He sent them to a printer. Within weeks, copies were circulating across Germany. Within months, across Europe. He followed them with a torrent of short, cheap pamphlets written not in Latin for theologians but in German for the common man: vivid, polemical, deliberately emotional. By 1523, there were 390 printings of his works in a single year.

Luther became the first person in history whose ideas traveled faster than any institution could contain them.

What his rise revealed was something more important than any single theological argument: the press had permanently decoupled the dissemination of a claim from any process of verifying it. The Church could no longer be the sole arbiter of what was sacred and what was heresy, because it no longer controlled who could speak or who could be heard.

Into that vacuum rushed everyone.

Catholic and Protestant factions built their own printing networks, their own pamphleteers, their own parallel accounts of the same events. Artists aligned with the Reformation produced woodcut prints depicting the Pope with a demon’s head, mass-produced and sold in marketplaces. The two sides didn’t merely disagree on theology... each inhabited a reality the other found not just wrong but monstrous. And when your information environment makes it feel self-evident that the other side is evil, the distance to violence shortens considerably.

Less hype, more thinking. For the people more interested in the pattern than the headline.

150 Years of Fire

The French Wars of Religion began in 1562 and lasted 36 years. Catholics and Protestant Huguenots tore France apart in conflicts so brutal that entire cities changed hands multiple times. Printed pamphlets ran ahead of every army, radicalizing populations before soldiers arrived and justifying massacres after they left. The St. Bartholomew’s Day Massacre of 1572, in which thousands of Huguenots were killed across France in a single wave of coordinated violence, was followed immediately by a flood of printed Catholic texts framing the slaughter as divine justice.

Then came the Thirty Years’ War.

Beginning in 1618 across the Holy Roman Empire (the sprawling, fragmented territory covering much of modern Germany, Austria, and Central Europe), it drew in nearly every major European power over three decades. What began as a religious conflict between Catholic and Protestant princes metastasized into something almost beyond comprehension. Armies grew enormous, sustained by systematic pillage of civilian populations. Disease and famine traveled in their wake. The Sack of Magdeburg in 1631 killed roughly 24,000 civilians in a single engagement, the majority burned alive in their homes. By the time the war ended in 1648, somewhere between four and eight million people were dead, and in some regions of Germany, a third of the entire population had simply ceased to exist.

These conflicts were, at their core, the violent consequence of a civilization that had lost its ability to share a common reality, with no institutions yet capable of building a new one.

We’ve Been Here Before

In the months following the 2020 United States presidential election, a specific claim spread across social media, cable news channels, and online forums: that the election had been stolen through widespread fraud. Election officials in every contested state investigated it. Dozens of courts reviewed it, including judges appointed by both political parties. Every single one rejected it for lack of evidence.

Yet by January 2021, a significant portion of the American public believed it as settled fact. On January 6th, thousands of people stormed the United States Capitol building, interrupting the certification of electoral votes and leaving five people dead. They were acting on the logical conclusion of an information environment where every platform they used, every channel they watched, every account they followed had spent months telling them the same thing, and where no shared arbiter existed whose authority they would accept, rather than on any single lie told by any single person.

During the same years, the COVID-19 pandemic produced a parallel fracture. The virus, its origins, the safety of vaccines, the legitimacy of public health measures... none of these remained questions to be answered by evidence. They became identity markers. In the United States, whether you wore a mask in a grocery store signaled your political tribe. Doctors and epidemiologists who had spent careers building credibility found themselves shouted down by anonymous accounts with no credentials and millions of followers. Two separate countries existed inside one geography, consuming entirely different sets of facts, incapable of agreeing on even the most basic empirical questions.

The 16th century had woodcut propaganda depicting the Pope with a demon’s head, mass-produced and distributed in marketplaces. We have algorithmically optimized content ecosystems, precision-engineered to maximize emotional engagement.

The delivery mechanism is different, but the underlying mechanics are identical: assertion dressed as fact, repetition manufacturing the illusion of consensus, and no legitimate institutional authority capable of settling disputes before they get worse.

How Europe Eventually Found Its Way Out

The Peace of Westphalia in 1648 ended the Thirty Years’ War, but calling it a triumph of reason would be generous. It was a treaty of sheer civilizational exhaustion. Nobody won. Millions were dead. The theological questions that had started the whole catastrophe remained unresolved. Westphalia was, essentially, an agreement to stop killing each other over them, buying time for something more lasting to emerge.

What followed, slowly and painfully, was a generation of thinkers trying to answer a genuinely hard question: if you can no longer trust the Church to tell you what is true, and you can’t trust your rulers, and you’ve watched your own senses be manipulated by propaganda for decades... then what exactly can you trust, and how do you build knowledge on top of it?

René Descartes approached this by deciding to trust nothing at all, at first. He systematically doubted everything he had ever been told: scripture, tradition, the testimony of others, until he arrived at the one thing he couldn’t doubt, that he himself was doing the doubting. From that single point of certainty, he tried to reconstruct a method for knowing things that didn’t depend on any authority that could be corrupted or hijacked. It was a direct response to living in a world where every authority had been discredited, dressed up as philosophy.

John Locke, writing in England later in the century, came at the same problem from a different angle. He observed that much of the sectarian violence around him was driven by people claiming direct, unmediated access to divine truth: revelations that couldn’t be questioned or tested. His answer was to argue that even claims of divine revelation must be evaluated by human reason. No assertion, however loudly made or widely repeated, was exempt from scrutiny. He was trying to establish a standard for settling disputes that didn’t end in bloodshed.

Pierre Bayle, a French Protestant refugee living in Holland, took a more practical route. He created what became known as the Arsenal of the Enlightenment: a vast Historical and Critical Dictionary that modeled, in exhaustive public detail, how to hold competing claims up to each other, expose their contradictions, and refuse to accept any of them simply on authority. He was, in effect, teaching his readers how to think about information rather than just consume it.

And then came the institutions. The Royal Society of London, founded in 1660, introduced something that seems obvious now but was genuinely revolutionary then: before a scientific claim could count as knowledge, it had to be examined, challenged, and verified by a community of peers. Findings were read aloud at meetings, debated, and only then published in the world’s first academic journal. The process of verification became as important as the claim itself, reintroducing the same friction that the printing press had so violently removed.

The Enlightenment wasn’t the spontaneous flowering of human reason, and it wasn’t the inevitable gift of the printing press. It was a deliberate, generational, painstaking effort to rebuild the institutions that told a civilization what counted as true, after those institutions had been destroyed by a technology nobody was ready for.

Europe took roughly 150 years and millions of lives to understand that you can’t suppress a new information technology... you can only build new institutions to manage it.
